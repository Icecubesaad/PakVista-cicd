Lahore – City of Gardens

A city where Mughal grandeur, colonial heritage, and modern vibrancy meet at every turn.

Introduction

Lahore, the capital of Punjab province, is Pakistan's cultural heart. With a history stretching back over a millennium, the city served as the jewel of the Mughal Empire and today remains the country's foremost centre of art, music, cuisine, and education. Its walled old city is a labyrinth of bazaars, shrines, and havelis that have barely changed in centuries, while modern Lahore pulses with universities, restaurants, and a vibrant startup scene.

Visitors to Lahore are immediately struck by the warmth of its people — the Lahoris are celebrated throughout Pakistan for their hospitality and love of celebration. Whether it is the fragrance of jasmine garlands at Data Darbar, the call to prayer echoing from the Badshahi Mosque, or the aroma of Lahori karahi drifting through Gawalmandi Food Street, Lahore is a city that engages all five senses simultaneously.

Badshahi Mosque

Completed in 1673 under the reign of Emperor Aurangzeb, the Badshahi Mosque is one of the largest mosques in the world and arguably the most recognisable symbol of Lahore. Its vast red sandstone courtyard can accommodate over 100,000 worshippers at a time, and its three marble domes and four minarets have dominated the Lahore skyline for more than three hundred and fifty years.

The mosque sits directly opposite the Lahore Fort, separated only by the Hazuri Bagh garden — a spatial arrangement that reflects the Mughal design philosophy of balancing power, faith, and nature. At dusk, when the floodlights illuminate its façade and the adhaan reverberates across the city, the Badshahi Mosque is an experience that stays with visitors for life.

Lahore Fort

The Lahore Fort, known locally as Shahi Qila (Royal Fort), is a UNESCO World Heritage Site that encapsulates over a thousand years of South Asian history within its 20-hectare walls. The fort was significantly expanded and embellished by successive Mughal emperors — most notably Akbar, Jahangir, Shah Jahan, and Aurangzeb — resulting in a complex that blends Persian, Timurid, and Punjabi architectural styles.

Among the fort's most celebrated features is the Sheesh Mahal (Palace of Mirrors), whose interior walls and ceilings are encrusted with thousands of tiny mirror fragments that scatter light into kaleidoscopic patterns. The Diwan-e-Aam (Hall of Public Audience) and the Moti Masjid (Pearl Mosque) are equally impressive, and the Alamgiri Gate — facing the Badshahi Mosque — is considered one of the finest gateways in Mughal architecture.

Shalimar Gardens

Laid out in 1641 under the orders of Emperor Shah Jahan, the Shalimar Gardens are a masterpiece of Mughal landscape design and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The gardens are arranged across three terraced levels, each representing a different aspect of paradise as described in Persian poetry — the highest level for private imperial enjoyment, the middle for the court, and the lowest for the public.

The gardens are famous for their 410 fountains fed by a sophisticated canal system that drew water from the River Ravi. Marble pavilions, ornate water channels, and carefully aligned fruit trees create an atmosphere of serene beauty that Shah Jahan intended as an earthly reflection of Quranic descriptions of paradise. In spring, when the roses and jasmine are in bloom and the fountains are in full flow, Shalimar Gardens offer one of the most tranquil experiences in all of South Asia.

Travel Tips for Lahore

Category Information
Best Time to Visit October to March (mild temperatures, outdoor sightseeing ideal)
Getting There Allama Iqbal International Airport (LHE) with direct flights from major cities
Local Transport Lahore Metro Bus, rickshaws, ride-hailing apps (InDrive, Careem)
Must Try Food Lahori Karahi, Paye, Nihari, Sohan Halwa, Kulfi Faluda
UNESCO Sites Lahore Fort and Shalimar Gardens (jointly inscribed 1981)
