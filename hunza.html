Hunza Valley

A mountain paradise in the heart of the Karakoram — where glaciers meet ancient forts and turquoise lakes.

Introduction

Nestled at an elevation of approximately 2,438 metres in the Gilgit-Baltistan region of northern Pakistan, Hunza Valley is widely regarded as one of the most beautiful places on Earth. Flanked by the Karakoram, Hindukush, and Himalayan mountain ranges, the valley offers a breathtaking panorama of snow-capped peaks, verdant terraced fields, and deep blue glacial rivers that has inspired travellers, writers, and explorers for centuries.

The people of Hunza — predominantly from the Burusho ethnic group, speaking the Burushaski language — are renowned for their longevity, literacy, and exceptional hospitality. The valley has one of the highest literacy rates in Pakistan and has produced generations of mountaineers, educators, and civil servants. Tourism has transformed the local economy in recent decades, with guesthouses, cafes, and adventure agencies lining the Karakoram Highway.

Rakaposhi Viewpoint

Rakaposhi, at 7,788 metres the 27th-highest peak in the world, dominates the eastern skyline of Hunza Valley with a presence that is both humbling and awe-inspiring. The mountain holds a Guinness World Record for the greatest uninterrupted rise from base to summit of any peak in the world — a vertical ascent of approximately 5,800 metres from the valley floor of the Hunza River.

The most accessible viewpoint for Rakaposhi is located at Rakaposhi Base Camp near the village of Minapin, approximately 40 kilometres south of Karimabad. On clear days — most reliably in the early morning before clouds gather — the mountain's massive south face is reflected in a small glacial lake, creating one of the most photographed scenes in northern Pakistan. The short trek to the base camp, through apricot orchards and pine forests, is manageable for most visitors and takes approximately 3–4 hours one way.

Attabad Lake

Attabad Lake is one of the most dramatic natural features in Pakistan — and one of the most unusual, given that it was created not by geological forces over millennia but by a catastrophic landslide in January 2010 that buried the village of Attabad and blocked the Hunza River for five months. When the debris dam was finally overtopped, it had created a 21-kilometre-long lake of stunning turquoise-blue water, now one of the most visited tourist attractions in Gilgit-Baltistan.

The lake's extraordinary colour — the result of fine glacial silt suspended in the water — contrasts dramatically with the ochre and brown mountains that rise sharply from its shores. Boat rides on the lake are the primary tourist activity, with local boatmen ferrying visitors across its surface for a close-up view of the submerged remains of the old Karakoram Highway. In summer, jet skiing, paddleboating, and kayaking are also available. The tunnels carved through the mountains to bypass the lake — a remarkable feat of engineering completed in 2015 — are themselves a tourist attraction, as vehicles drive through 5 kilometres of rock hewn by hand and machine.

Baltit Fort

Perched dramatically on a rocky outcrop above the town of Karimabad, Baltit Fort has watched over Hunza Valley for approximately 700 years. Originally built around the 14th century and substantially renovated in the 17th century by a Balti princess who brought craftsmen from Baltistan to redesign it in the Tibetan architectural style, the fort served as the residence of the Mirs (rulers) of Hunza until 1945.

The Aga Khan Trust for Culture undertook a major restoration of Baltit Fort in the 1990s, preserving its original structure while installing interpretive displays that tell the story of Hunza's royal history. Visitors can explore the fort's wooden-beamed rooms, dark storage chambers, the Mir's audience hall, a dungeon used for prisoners, and a rooftop observation deck that offers one of the finest panoramic views in all of Pakistan — with Rakaposhi gleaming to the south and the peaks of the Karakoram stretching in every direction.

Travel Tips for Hunza Valley

Category Information
Best Time to Visit April–May (cherry blossoms) and September–October (clear skies, autumn colours)
Getting There Fly to Gilgit Airport then drive via Karakoram Highway (approx. 2 hours to Karimabad)
Local Transport Suzuki vans, hired jeeps, walking — the valley is compact and walkable
Must Try Food Chapshoro (meat pastry), Dirams (buckwheat crepes), Apricot oil, Hunza water (local wine)
Altitude Note Karimabad sits at ~2,400 m; allow a day to acclimatise before trekking
