Karachi – City of Lights

Pakistan's largest metropolis — a coastal megacity where commerce, culture, and the Arabian Sea converge.

Introduction

Karachi is Pakistan's economic engine, its largest city, and its primary seaport — a sprawling coastal metropolis of over 20 million people that generates approximately half of Pakistan's total tax revenue and handles over 60% of the nation's international trade. Founded as a small fishing village known as Kolachi in the 18th century, Karachi grew rapidly under British colonial rule, becoming the first capital of independent Pakistan in 1947 before the seat of government was transferred to Islamabad in the 1960s.

Today Karachi is one of the world's largest cities by population and one of the most diverse — home to speakers of dozens of languages, followers of every major religion, and communities with roots across South Asia, the Middle East, and beyond. Its energy is unmistakable: the city never truly sleeps, and its food street culture, art scene, and entrepreneurial spirit make it a destination unlike any other in Pakistan.

Clifton Beach

Clifton Beach, officially known as Sea View, is Karachi's most popular public beach, stretching for several kilometres along the Arabian Sea coast in the upscale Clifton neighbourhood. For Karachi residents — particularly those who may never have the opportunity to visit the mountains or the valleys of the north — Clifton Beach is the city's great recreational escape, drawing millions of visitors each year, especially on weekends and holidays.

The beach is lined with camel and horse rides for children, food stalls selling bhutta (roasted corn), gola ganda (shaved ice), and fresh coconut water, as well as fairground rides and souvenir sellers. At sunset, the sky above the Arabian Sea turns a spectacular deep orange and pink, and the beach fills with families, couples, and groups of friends who come to watch the light fade over the water. The nearby Clifton promenade and the elegant buildings of the old Clifton cantonment area give the neighbourhood a distinct character that sets it apart from the rest of the city.

Mohatta Palace

Mohatta Palace is one of the finest examples of colonial-era architecture in Pakistan and arguably Karachi's most beautiful historic building. Constructed in 1927 for the wealthy Rajasthani merchant Shivratan Chandraratan Mohatta, the palace was designed in a style that fuses Rajput, Mughal, and Indo-Saracenic architectural traditions — a building that looks as though it belongs simultaneously to Jodhpur and to Karachi.

The palace is built of pink Jodhpur sandstone and yellow Gizri stone, giving it a warm, rose-gold appearance that catches the afternoon light beautifully. After partition in 1947, the palace was occupied by Fatima Jinnah, the sister of Pakistan's founder Muhammad Ali Jinnah, who lived here until her death in 1967. The Sindh government subsequently converted it into a museum of art and culture, which today hosts rotating exhibitions of Pakistani contemporary art, Sindhi handicrafts, and historical artefacts. The palace's ornate interior — its carved arches, stained glass windows, and original Rajasthani tilework — makes it one of the most photographed interiors in Karachi.

Pakistan Maritime Museum

The Pakistan Maritime Museum, located in the Clifton area near the main KPT (Karachi Port Trust) complex, is a dedicated institution celebrating Pakistan's naval history, maritime heritage, and the strategic importance of the Arabian Sea to the nation's economy and security. Established in 1988 by the Pakistan Navy, the museum spans an outdoor and indoor collection spread across several hectares.

The outdoor exhibits are the museum's most striking feature: visitors can walk around and board several decommissioned naval vessels, including a submarine, a destroyer, and a gunboat. The sight of a full-sized submarine sitting on the grounds of a museum, available for public exploration, is one that surprises and delights visitors of all ages. Indoor galleries contain model ships, navigational instruments, maps, uniforms, and weapons tracing Pakistan's maritime history from ancient Indus Valley river trade to the modern Pakistan Navy. The museum is particularly popular with school groups and families with young children, for whom the chance to climb inside a real submarine is an unforgettable experience.

Travel Tips for Karachi

Category Information
Best Time to Visit November to February (cooler temperatures, low humidity)
Getting There Jinnah International Airport (KHI) — Pakistan's busiest airport, direct international flights
Local Transport Ride-hailing apps (Careem, InDrive), BRT Green Line, yellow cabs, rickshaws
Must Try Food Biryani, Bun Kebab, Karachi Haleem, Seekh Kebab, Lassi at Burns Road
Safety Note Karachi is generally safe for tourists in established areas; use ride-hailing apps after dark
